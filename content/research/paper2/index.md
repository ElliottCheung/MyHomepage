##### Abstract

The sharing economy has gained tremendous popularity in the past decade, but little is known about providers who join these platforms out of intrinsic interest. Drawing on a novel entrepreneurial passion perspective, this study theorizes the underlying logic by which intrinsic interest gives rise to distinct behavioral patterns among sharing-economy providers, and examines the potential value and risks that interest-driven providers may pose for platforms. Using a proprietary dataset spanning more than two years from a major meal sharing platform, we find that interest driven providers tend to enter the market earlier, offer dishes similar to those of nearby competitors, and introduce new dishes more frequently. Over time, their imitative positioning remains relatively stable, whereas their innovative activity declines significantly. Moreover, our results indicate that interest driven providers have higher market retention rates, a pattern driven primarily by superior performance rather than by long term commitment rooted in their initial interest. Notably, we identify a strategic pathway that can mitigate the waning of entrepreneurial passion: interest driven providers who undertake more frequent innovation focused pivots during their entrepreneurial trajectory are more likely to sustain their passion for platform entrepreneurship over the long term. These findings yield important implications for market coordination and operational practices on sharing economy platforms
